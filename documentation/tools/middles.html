What is a Middle?

A middle occurs when you can bet both sides of a point spread or total at different sportsbooks, creating a range where both bets can win. If the final result lands in this "middle" range, you win both bets for a significant profit. If it lands outside the range, you typically lose a small amount (the juice/vig).

Middle Example

NFL Game: Chiefs vs. Bills

  • Sportsbook A: Chiefs -6.5 (-110)
  • Sportsbook B: Bills +7.5 (-110)
  • Middle Range: Chiefs win by exactly 7 points
  • Result if Middle Hits: Both bets win for massive profit
  • Result if Middle Misses: Small loss (just the juice)

Types of Middles

üèà Point Spread Middles

Bet both sides of a game at different spreads, profiting when the margin of victory falls between the two lines.

üìä Total Points Middles

üéØ Player Prop Middles

Middle Betting Strategy

üéØ Key Numbers in Sports

Certain numbers are more likely to occur as final margins, making them valuable middle targets:

üèà NFL Key Numbers

  • 3 Points: Most common margin (field goal)
  • 7 Points: Touchdown difference
  • 10 Points: Touchdown + field goal
  • 14 Points: Two touchdown difference

üèÄ NBA Key Numbers

  • 1-3 Points: Close games, free throws
  • 5-8 Points: Common final margins
  • 12-15 Points: Multiple possession games

‚öæ MLB Totals

  • 7-9 Runs: Most common game totals
  • 10-12 Runs: Higher scoring games
  • Even Numbers: Slightly more common than odd

üí∞ Bankroll Management for Middles

  • Risk Assessment: Calculate maximum loss before placing bets
  • Unit Sizing: Use consistent bet sizes relative to bankroll
  • Volume Requirements: Need sufficient bets for positive results
  • Profit Targets: Set realistic expectations for middle success
Pro Tip: Focus on middles with key numbers in the range. A 3-point middle in NFL (e.g., -2.5/-5.5) has much higher hit probability than a 3-point middle at uncommon numbers.
