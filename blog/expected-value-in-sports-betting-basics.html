Expected Value in Sports Betting: Basics

Expected Value (EV) is the key to smarter sports betting. It helps you calculate the average profit or loss you can expect over time based on probabilities, not emotions. Here's what you need to know:

  • What is EV?
    EV measures whether a bet is profitable in the long run. It compares your probability of an event happening to the sportsbook's implied probability.
  • Why does EV matter?

  • How to calculate EV:
    Use this formula:
    EV = (Win Probability × Payout) - (Loss Probability × Stake)

What is Expected Value in Sports Betting?

Expected Value Basics

Expected Value (EV) helps you make smarter betting decisions by calculating the average outcomes over time based on probabilities.

What Expected Value Means

At its core, EV compares your estimated probability of an event happening to the sportsbook's implied probability. Let’s break it down with an example: if a sportsbook lists the Dallas Cowboys at +100 odds, they’re implying a 50% chance of winning. But if your research suggests the Cowboys have a 60% chance, you’ve spotted a positive EV opportunity. That 10% difference becomes your edge.

Component Your Assessment Sportsbook Implied EV (per $100 wager)
Probability 60% 50% Positive
Risk/Reward -$100 +$100 +$20

This simple calculation is the foundation for creating profitable betting strategies over time.

Impact on Long-Term Results

Grasping EV is key to long-term success. Betting on positive EV opportunities isn’t just about luck - it’s the only mathematically grounded approach to sustained profitability. While individual bets can swing either way, consistently betting with positive EV stacks the odds in your favor over time.

Here’s a quick example: imagine a coin flip game where you win $11 for heads but lose $10 for tails. The odds are even, but the EV works out to (0.5 × $11) – (0.5 × $10) = $0.50 per flip. Over many flips, that small edge adds up.

Professional bettors focus on EV rather than just their win-loss record because it’s a better indicator of long-term profitability. For instance, a bettor winning 45% of their wagers by picking high-value underdogs could earn more than someone winning 55% on low-value bets. Even occasional wins on negative EV bets can’t offset the built-in disadvantage from the vig or juice, which ensures those bets lose money over time.

EV Calculation Steps

Here's how to calculate Expected Value (EV) step by step.

Basic EV Formula

The formula for Expected Value looks like this:

EV = (Win Probability × Payout) - (Loss Probability × Stake)

To use this formula effectively, follow these steps:

  • Convert the odds into implied probability.
  • Compare the implied probability with your own probability assessment.
  • Calculate the potential payout.
  • Determine the stake amount.
  • Plug the values into the formula.

Sample Calculation

Let’s break it down with an example: A $100 bet on the Brooklyn Nets at +200 odds, with a 45% win probability.

Item Value Calculation
Odds +200 Implies a 33.3% win probability
Your Assessment 45% -
Potential Win $200 Based on +200 odds for $100
Potential Loss $100 Stake amount
Loss Probability 55% 100% - 45%

Now, substitute these values into the EV formula:

EV = (0.45 × $200) - (0.55 × $100)
EV = $90 - $55
EV = $35

This positive EV of $35 indicates that, on average, you could expect to earn $35 for every $100 wagered on similar bets over the long term.

EV Calculation Errors to Avoid

Probability Assessment Mistakes

Misjudging probabilities is a common pitfall in EV betting. Many bettors let emotions cloud their judgment, often overestimating their favorite team's chances of winning. This bias usually comes from personal attachment rather than an objective look at the numbers.

Take this example: A bettor sees the New York Giants listed at +200 odds, which translates to an implied probability of 33.3%. But, swayed by recent hype or media coverage, they estimate the team's chances at 50%. If the true probability is closer to 35%, this inflated estimate creates a false sense of positive EV.

Here’s how to avoid common probability assessment errors:

Assessment Error Impact Prevention Strategy
Confirmation Bias Focusing only on data that supports personal beliefs Use neutral data sources and rely on statistical models.
Emotional Attachment Overrating a team due to loyalty or bias Compare your estimates with market odds and expert tools.
Intuition Over Evidence Disregarding hard data in favor of gut feelings Base decisions on historical stats and advanced analytics.

Accurately assessing probabilities is just one piece of the puzzle. Equally important is how you manage your betting bankroll.

Money Management Issues

Even with sharp probability estimates, poor bankroll management can lead to unnecessary losses. Successful bettors rely on disciplined, math-based strategies rather than gut instincts.

Here’s how to keep your bankroll in check:

  • Stick to Fixed Percentage Staking: Methods like the Kelly Criterion or flat betting work well. Limit each wager to about 1–2% of your total bankroll.
  • Avoid Chasing Losses: Don’t increase bet sizes to recover from a losing streak. Stay consistent.
  • Track Your Performance: Regularly compare your probability estimates with actual outcomes. This helps identify patterns and fix recurring mistakes.

Betting on too many positive EV opportunities at once can also be risky. Even with an edge, downswings are inevitable, and overextending yourself could lead to significant losses. The solution? Stay disciplined, prioritize quality over quantity, and focus on long-term growth.

Variance is a natural part of sports betting. Even well-researched bets can lose in the short term. However, by sticking to strict bankroll management, you’ll be better equipped to handle these swings and maximize your edge over time.
